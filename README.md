# surveillance-based-harassment

Surveillance-based harassment involves the persistent, unwanted, or covert monitoring, tracking, and observing of an individual to cause distress, limit their freedom, or exert control [[29]]. Often referred to as "tech-facilitated abuse," "cyberstalking," or "coercive control," this behavior uses both physical tools (like hidden cameras) and digital tools (like spyware or location tracking) to stalk or harass a victim [[31]].

While the core concept is similar across borders, the legal mechanisms used to prosecute it differ in the United Kingdom, Ireland, and the European Union.

### 1. United Kingdom (UK)
In the UK, surveillance-based harassment is typically prosecuted under harassment and domestic abuse laws, as well as data protection regulations.
*   **Protection from Harassment Act 1997:** This is the primary legislation used to prosecute harassment, making it illegal for anyone to repeatedly follow, monitor, or threaten another person [[7]]. This includes digital monitoring, such as hacking into accounts or using tracking devices.
*   **Coercive or Controlling Behavior:** The Serious Crime Act 2015 made "coercive control" a criminal offense in intimate or family relationships. This explicitly covers "digitally enabled coercive control," which includes excessive surveillance of a victim’s communications, movements, and activities [[13]].
*   **Neighbor Surveillance and Data Protection:** In civil or neighbor disputes, the domestic use of CCTV can be challenged as harassment under the 1997 Act if it is directed at a neighbor’s private life [[1]]. In a landmark case (*Fairhurst v Woodard*), a neighbor successfully sued a household for harassment and breaches of the UK GDPR due to the overreach of their home security cameras [[61]].

### 2. Ireland (IE)
In Ireland, harassment and surveillance are addressed through a combination of non-fatal offenses legislation and modernized domestic violence laws.
*   **Non-Fatal Offences Against the Person Act 1997:** Section 10 of this Act defines and criminalizes harassment, which includes persistently watching, following, or monitoring someone in a way that seriously alarms or distresses them [[42]].
*   **Domestic Violence Act 2018:** This landmark legislation introduced the specific criminal offense of "coercive control" in Ireland [[51]]. This law recognizes that psychological abuse, including the use of surveillance devices in the home or the constant monitoring of a partner’s location, is a form of domestic violence [[22]].
*   **Harmful Communications:** Under "Coco’s Law" (Harassment, Harmful Communications and Related Offences Act 2020), stalking and harassment conducted via social media or electronic communication are treated as serious criminal offenses [[23]].

### 3. European Union (EU)
The EU addresses surveillance-based harassment through a combination of fundamental privacy rights, data protection laws, and directives targeting gender-based violence.
*   **General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR):** This regulation establishes strict rules on how personal data is collected and processed across Europe [[67]]. The use of hidden cameras, unauthorized audio recording, or "stalkerware" that collects a person's data without their consent is a direct violation of their GDPR rights and fundamental privacy.
*   **Cyber-Violence Directives:** The EU has strengthened its legal frameworks to combat "tech-facilitated abuse" as part of its directive on combating violence against women and domestic violence [[40]]. This includes criminalizing cyberstalking and the non-consensual use of surveillance technologies.
*   **Prevalence and Monitoring:** EU-wide surveys have highlighted that surveillance is a pervasive issue, with data indicating that over 10% of women have had their location monitored or tracked by a partner [[36]]. The European Parliament continues to address how emerging technologies, such as deepfakes and location-tracking apps, fuel a rise in digital harassment [[38]].

### Common Forms of Surveillance-Based Harassment
Across these jurisdictions, authorities and support organizations recognize several specific tactics:
*   **Stalkerware/Spyware:** Installing apps on a victim’s phone to read their messages, view their photos, or track their GPS location.
*   **Smart Home Abuse:** Abusers using smart speakers, connected baby monitors, or smart thermostats to listen in on conversations or manipulate the victim's home environment.
*   **GPS Tracking:** Using devices like Apple AirTags or hidden vehicle trackers to monitor a victim's physical movements.
*   **Account Compromise:** Unauthorized access to email or social media accounts to monitor the victim’s interactions and isolate them from friends and family.
